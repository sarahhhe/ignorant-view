The Credit Card Snowball Effect

I came across a credit repaying concept called the snowball effect when I was knee deep in personal finance material. The concept instructs debt re-payers to pay off their lowest debt first by contributing as much as possible. Higher total credit cards will be paid the minimum amount.

The advantage of this approach is purely psychological. The feeling of removing the debt of a credit card provides a motivational boost to carry on. The disadvantage is credit cards with a higher total grow at a faster rate, causing more money to be paid off.

Though the disadvantage is clear and greater than the advantage, the credit card snowball is one of the most effective techniques for removing debt. Money is tightly coupled with our psyche.

The psychological impact is profound and it can encourage us to chase the feeling of being debt free. Trying to pay off our highest debt can make us feel swamped and stuck. Eliminating our smallest sum gives us a sense of accomplishment and positive reinforcement.

I have three accounts with debt:

  • Natwest: £1400 7% APR
  • Tymit :£450 at 24% APR
  • Paypal: £70 at 24% APR

I have taken the approach to pay my lowest credit card first. Additionally, I have set up credit agreements with my account providers that make my repayment flexible and affordable.

My Debt Repayment Reasoning

My first debt to be paid off is Paypal, as it is only £70. Next, my credit agreement with my bank provider gave me the option to reduce my APR and pay in three instalments, getting rid of that second. Lastly, Tymit has a flexible policy where the first three months have no interest. In my repayment strategy, I plan to take advantage of that as much as possible.

The snowball effect is a great strategy for those who feel daunted by their high debt. I have found that using the snowball effect and using the financial repayment options provided by the bank is the best option as it mitigates the drawbacks of the snowball approach.
